Gateway of India

The Gateway of India is a monumental stone arch built on the Mumbai waterfront at Apollo Bunder to commemorate the 1911 visit of King George V and Queen Mary. Designed by Scottish architect George Wittet, the structure stands 26 metres (85 feet) tall and is a prime example of Indo-Saracenic architecture, blending Indian, Islamic, and European design elements with intricate stone latticework known as jaalis. Although the foundation was laid in 1911, the actual construction was completed in 1924 using yellow basalt and reinforced concrete. Historically, it served as a ceremonial entrance for British officials, but it gained its most significant symbolic weight on February 28, 1948, when the last British troops departed India through its arches, marking the end of colonial rule. Today, it remains Mumbai’s most visited landmark, serving as the main boarding point for ferries to the Elephanta Caves and a lively social hub overlooking the Arabian Sea. Beyond its historical role, the monument has evolved into a vibrant cultural center that hosts annual Hanukkah celebrations for Mumbai's Jewish community and serves as a backdrop for numerous iconic Bollywood films

Golden Temple

The Golden Temple, or Sri Harmandir Sahib, is the preeminent spiritual shrine of Sikhism, located in the holy city of Amritsar, Punjab. Founded in 1577 by Guru Ram Das and completed in 1604 by Guru Arjan Dev, the temple is built in the center of the sacred Amrit Sarovar lake. Its iconic appearance, featuring a fluted dome covered in over 400 kg of gold leaf, was commissioned in 1830 by Maharaja Ranjit Singh. The temple's unique architecture, including four entrances, symbolises universal brotherhood, while its Guru Ka Langar kitchen serves free meals to over 100,000 people daily, embodying the core Sikh values of equality and service. Complementing this spiritual sanctuary is the Akal Takht, which stands opposite the sanctum as the highest seat of temporal authority, ensuring a perfect balance between spiritual guidance and social justice for the global Sikh community.Additionally, the complex houses the Central Sikh Museum, which meticulously chronicles the rich history and martyrdom of the Sikh Gurus through rare artifacts and paintings. This ensures that every visitor not only experiences the temple's serenity but also gains a profound understanding of the resilience and heritage of the Sikh faith.

Mysuru Palace

The Mysore Palace stands as a breathtaking testament to India's royal heritage and architectural brilliance. Located in the heart of Mysuru, the current structure was designed by the British architect Henry Irwin and completed in 1912 after the original wooden palace was tragically destroyed by fire. It is a masterpiece of the Indo-Saracenic style, seamlessly blending Hindu, Mughal, Rajput, and Gothic elements. The palace exterior is characterized by its majestic pink marble domes and a soaring five-story tower, but the interior is where the true opulence lies; visitors are greeted by the Kalyana Mantapa (wedding hall) with its vibrant stained-glass ceilings and the grand Durbar Hall, which features intricately carved pillars and gold-leaf paintings.Beyond its physical beauty, the palace serves as the cultural soul of Karnataka, especially during the annual Dasara festival, when it is adorned with nearly 100,000 light bulbs, transforming it into a glowing golden beacon. As the seat of the Wadiyar dynasty, the palace remains a living museum, housing a vast collection of royal memorabilia, weaponry, and the legendary Golden Throne, making it one of the most iconic and visited landmarks in South India.
